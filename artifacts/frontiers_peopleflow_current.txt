 2   ABSTRACT
 3    Evaluating evacuation performance in complex buildings requires analysis methods that capture
 4   interactions between spatial configuration, pedestrian behavior, and operational disruptions.
 5   This paper introduces PeopleFlow, a layout-informed computational framework for structured
 6   evacuation-safety comparison across diverse architectural layouts. The workflow converts floor-
 7   plan representations into navigable simulation environments and applies continuous-space
 8   multi-agent modeling based on force-driven pedestrian dynamics. PeopleFlow reports inter-
 9   pretable indicators including total clearance duration, localized congestion intensity, bottleneck
10   persistence, and exit-utilization balance. To enable consistent experimentation, the framework
11   uses parameterized scenario configurations and reproducibility-oriented execution records. The
12   evaluation covers a structured matrix of nine layout types across five operational conditions (450
13   core runs), plus 210 controlled supplementary/sensitivity runs for real-layout generalization and
14   sensitivity to exit width, desired speed, and occupancy scaling. Results show strong depende-
15   nce of evacuation outcomes on geometric topology and routing strategy, with clearance-time
16   differences exceeding an eightfold range and substantial congestion variation across layouts.
17   The primary contribution is a reproducible engineering framework for relative safety prioritization
18   across heterogeneous architectural configurations, rather than deterministic prediction of indivi-
19   dual trajectories. The framework supports what-if analysis during early design and provides a
20   computational basis for integration with digital twin workflows for high-occupancy safety planning.
21   From a built-environment perspective, the framework enables early-stage design evaluation of
22   evacuation efficiency and supports decision-making in smart building planning, infrastructure
23   safety assessment, and digital-twin-based emergency management systems.
24 Keywords: Crowd simulation, evacuation analytics, evacuation modelling, digital twin, multi-agent modelling, multi-agent simulation,
25 smart infrastructure, building evacuation safety, reproducible pipeline.

     1     INTRODUCTION

26   Building evacuation performance evaluation is a high-impact engineering task where static
27   compliance checks often miss dynamic crowd effects under disruption. Historical emergencies
28   consistently show that bottleneck formation, uneven exit utilization, and localized panic response
29   can dominate outcomes even in code-compliant facilities. Conventional hand calculations remain
30   useful for baseline checks, but they provide limited support for comparing design alternatives
31   under multi-hazard and operational uncertainty Hurley et al. (2016); Ronchi and Nilsson (2013).

32   Recent high-density infrastructure such as airports, metro systems, hospitals, and large comme-
33 rcial complexes introduces complex evacuation dynamics that cannot be fully evaluated using
34 static compliance rules alone.

35     The need for early-stage design validation has increased with higher occupancy density in
36   modern infrastructure. Although simulation enables repeatable structured comparison before
37   deployment, the central challenge is integrating architectural input, scenario control, execution, and
38   interpretable outputs within one coherent computational workflow. Such integrated computational
39   approaches are increasingly central to smart construction and digital built-environment systems,
40   where simulation-driven insights support safety-oriented design and operational decision-making.

41   The present research introduces PeopleFlow as a layout-informed evacuation efficiency framework
42 with traceable computational pipeline execution. Rather than proposing a new pedestrian-physics
43 law in isolation, the study develops a unified computational pipeline for robust scenario-based
44 evaluation across heterogeneous layouts.

45       The main contributions are summarized as follows:

46       • A reproducible floor-plan-aware simulation workflow linking layout ingestion, scenario
47         execution, and artifact-backed reporting.
48       • A structured cross-layout evacuation comparison across 660 simulations (450 core + 210
49         supplementary/sensitivity) spanning diverse building archetypes.
50       • A density-aware routing evaluation framework under structured stress scenarios, supported by
51         ablation and sensitivity analysis.

52     While prior evacuation simulators emphasize pedestrian behavioral realism or scenario-specific
53   visualization, they largely lack automated floor-plan ingestion, reproducible batch execution,
54   and artifact-native cross-layout benchmarking within a single coherent pipeline. PeopleFlow
55   addresses this gap directly. To the best of our knowledge, this is one of the first structured
56   openly reproducible framework that couples automated floor-plan ingestion, stress-scenario batch
57   execution, and artifact-native auditing for cross-layout evacuation-safety ranking – without
58   requiring manual geometry preparation or proprietary tooling. The contribution is methodological
59   and infrastructural: a transparent comparative-evaluation pipeline that enables architects, safety
60   engineers, and digital-twin integrators to systematically prioritize safer design alternatives under
61   structured stress conditions.

      2     RELATED WORK
 62   2.1   Crowd Modeling Approaches
 63     Pedestrian evacuation research spans both continuous and discrete formulations. In force-
 64   based formulations, evacuees can be represented as self-driven particles whose motion evolves
 65   through interaction potentials capturing repulsion, attraction, and directional preference; this
 66   family remains a physically interpretable baseline for dense-crowd analysis Helbing and Molnar
 67   (1995); Helbing et al. (2000). Cellular automata and floor-field variants offer efficient large-
 68   scale approximations but may introduce directional discretization artifacts near bottlenecks and
 69   tight corners Nishinari et al. (2004); Kirchner and Schadschneider (2002). Comparative studies
 70   indicate that model suitability is task-dependent; for complex indoor geometries, continuous-
 71   space approaches often preserve turning behavior and local interaction structure more naturally
 72   Zheng et al. (2009); Duives et al. (2013); Still (2014). Recent pedestrian-trajectory literature
 73   further improves interaction modeling using benchmark datasets and social-context forecasting
 74   architectures, including ETH/UCY-aligned methods based on recurrent, graph, and generative
 75   formulations Pellegrini et al. (2009); Alahi et al. (2016); Gupta et al. (2018); Salzmann et al.
 76   (2020); Mohamed et al. (2020); Zhou et al. (2021); Chen et al. (2021); Li et al. (2020); Wang et al.
 77   (2023); Saleh (2022).

 78   2.2   Evacuation Simulation Systems
 79     Commercial and academic evacuation platforms generally cover geometry authoring, route
 80   simulation, and report generation, but they differ substantially in automation depth, extensibility,
 81   and experiment traceability Gwynne et al. (1999); Ronchi and Gwynne (2015); Lovreglio et al.
 82   (2020). Behavior-focused studies further show that guidance logic, uncertainty handling, and
 83   panic-modulated decisions can materially alter evacuation outcomes Pan et al. (2007); Chen and
 84   Wang (2018). In many practical settings, heavy manual preprocessing and proprietary tooling still
 85   constrain transparent cross-layout benchmarking.

 86   2.3   Digital Twin Safety Analysis
 87     Digital twin studies in the built environment increasingly target safety and emergency operations.
 88   Prior work supports simulation-coupled twins for risk-aware planning, yet complete pipelines
 89   from floor-plan ingestion to repeatable quantitative evacuation indicators remain limited in many
 90   deployments Batty (2012); Zhang et al. (2021); Ozturk (2021); Osama (2024). Recent BIM-tied
 91   fire-safety and emergency-digital-twin systems also demonstrate practical monitoring, evacuation
 92   analytics, and control integration, reinforcing the need for end-to-end reproducible computational
 93   workflows Sun and Turkan (2020); Wehbe and Shahrour (2021); Ding et al. (2023); Jiang et al.
 94   (2023); Jahangir et al. (2025). These trends remain active in 2024–2026 deployment-oriented safety
 95   literature, with emphasis shifting from isolated twin prototypes toward interoperable, reproducible
 96   engineering workflows that can be independently rerun and audited.

100 reproducible framework rather than a claim of absolute predictive certainty. Its novelty lies in

      3     METHODOLOGY

117   3.1    Workflow Pipeline

118     PeopleFlow follows a deterministic workflow with four stages: (1) floor-plan ingestion, (2)
119   geometry extraction and navigation graph generation, (3) scenario-based simulation execution,
120   and (4) artifact-backed analytics. Each run is stored with configuration metadata, seeds, and
121   per-step metrics so results are replayable and auditable. The step-level simulation procedure is
122   summarized in Fig. 4.

123   3.2    Reproducibility Design

124       Each experiment is represented by a session artifact that captures:

125       • Input layout identifier and geometry hash.
126       • Scenario parameters (occupancy, blocked exits, routing policy, panic level).
127       • Random seed and simulation step size.
128       • Aggregated outputs and raw trajectory summaries.

129   This structure allows direct reruns and supports batch-level statistical comparisons. The
130   modular organization also enables independent evaluation of routing logic without modif-
131   ying geometric preprocessing components. Simulation configurations and synthetic benchmark
132   layouts are packaged as reproducible experiment artifacts and can be released to support

137   3.3   Agent Decision Logic

138     At each simulation step, agents execute a compact perception-decision-motion loop. This
139   structure ensures behavioral consistency while allowing routing-policy variation to be isolated
140   during controlled ablation experiments. Local density and queue conditions are sensed first, then
141   exit costs are updated using Eq. (5). The lowest-cost exit is selected, social-force dynamics are
142   integrated, and state variables are updated for the next step.

143   3.4   Computational Complexity

144     The computational complexity of a single simulation step is O(N k), where N is the number of
145   active agents and k is the average number of neighboring agents considered in local interaction-
146   force updates. Under practical density regimes, k remains locally bounded, giving near-linear
147   scaling with population size for each step. For batch execution, total workload scales as O(RN S),
148   where R is the number of runs and S is the number of simulation steps per run. For a layered
149   3D extension with inter-floor connectors, the step cost becomes O(N k + M ) where M is the

157   For a layered multi-floor extension, we define an inter-layer transition cost for moving from floor

160   This term is added to Eq. (5) when inter-floor routing alternatives are available.

165   The architecture consists of geometry ingestion, simulation kernel, metrics engine, and reporting
166 layer. Geometry ingestion transforms floor plans into obstacle and exit primitives. The simulation
167 kernel executes force-based motion and route updates. The metrics engine computes clearance,
168 density, bottleneck, and utilization indicators. The reporting layer produces figures and tables

198   5.3   Exit Selection Cost Function

199    For congestion-aware routing, the exit cost at time t is:

                                         Ce (t) = αde (t) + βρe (t) + γhe (t),                                   (5)

200   where de is path distance to exit e, ρe is local crowd density near e, and he is optional hazard
201   penalty. In this work, α and β control the relative influence of distance minimization versus
202   congestion avoidance, enabling evaluation of routing sensitivity under density-aware decision
203   policies. The routing formulation balances geometric shortest-path preference with local density
204   avoidance, enabling dynamic redistribution of agents across available exits under congestion
205   conditions. In the core and supplementary experiments, congestion-aware routing uses α = 1.0,
206   β = 2.0, and γ = 0.0 (hazard term disabled), with a local queue-perception radius of 5.0 m;
207   stochastic routing uses the same base cost with softmax temperature T = 15.0.

      6     EXPERIMENTAL SETUP
214   6.1    Simulation Parameters
215     The core study executes 450 runs (9 layouts × 5 scenarios × 10 repetitions). To strengthen
216   generalization and engineering interpretability, we additionally conduct 210 controlled runs:
217   150 supplementary real-layout runs (airport terminal, metro station, office building; full S1–S5
218   coverage), 30 exit-width comparison runs, and 30 desired-speed sensitivity runs. Key simulation
219   parameters are shown in Table 4. Nine layouts were selected to balance geometric diversity with
220   computational feasibility while enabling statistically meaningful cross-layout comparison under a
221   fixed 10-seed protocol and reproducible runtime budget. Parameter ranges were selected based
222   on commonly reported pedestrian-dynamics studies describing typical free-flow speeds between
223   1.2–1.5 m/s and density thresholds consistent with established fundamental-diagram behavior.

275   8.1    Main Multi-Layout Results

276   Table 8 reports means and standard deviations for evacuation time and peak density across all

278 baseline scenarios, clearance time ranges from 24.18 s (“Mall Plan”) to 182.68 s (“Academic Plan”),

282   ranking stability: layouts with corridor concentration remain systematically slower and denser

284   95% confidence intervals preserve the same high-risk and low-risk layout groups, indicating that
285   comparative conclusions are robust to stochastic variation even when absolute outcomes shift by
286   seed. This supports the intended use of the framework for relative safety prioritization rather than

426   9.5   Interpretation for Safety Engineering

427     The key finding is layout sensitivity under stress. Identical behavioral parameters can produce
428   large outcome shifts due to geometric topology and exit accessibility. For practitioners, this
429   means that design alternatives should be compared through structured scenario matrices rather
430   than a single nominal run. A focused Mall Plan mini-sweep varying a group-cohesion parameter
431   (weak-to-strong cohesion settings) showed consistent clearance delay and bottleneck persistence
432   growth as cohesion increased, reinforcing the need to include social-group effects in safety-focused
433   scenario studies.
434   The strongest practical use of PeopleFlow is comparative ranking: identifying which layout-policy
435 pair reduces extreme congestion and improves clearance robustness. This aligns with modern
436 risk-aware design practices and digital twin safety analytics Haghani (2020); Kuligowski (2013).

437     The proposed workflow enables architects and safety engineers to evaluate evacuation performance
438   before construction or retrofitting. Rather than relying solely on compliance rules, designers can
439   compare alternative layouts under structured stress scenarios and identify configurations that
440   minimize congestion risk. The framework also supports integration into digital twin systems for
441   continuous safety monitoring in smart buildings. Representative application domains include
442   smart-building safety audits, fire evacuation planning, airport and rail-concourse safety design,
443   emergency preparedness drills, and digital-twin what-if simulation. PeopleFlow can support
444   safety certification workflows, smart building design, and evacuation planning in high-occupancy
445   infrastructure.
446     This deployment pattern is particularly relevant for high-occupancy facilities such as transporta-
447   tion hubs, hospitals, and commercial centers, where transient surges in occupancy can quickly
448   produce unsafe crowd states if geometric and routing constraints are not evaluated in advance.
449   For multi-floor extension, the same routing logic can be lifted to a layered navigation graph in
450   which each floor is a graph layer and stairs/elevators are modeled as inter-layer connectors with
451   vertical-transition penalties. This preserves the scenario-comparison workflow while extending
452   applicability to 3D circulation networks.

456    1. Build a baseline model from the latest floor plan and verify extracted exits and obstacles.
457    2. Execute structured stress scenarios (high occupancy, blocked exits, routing-policy alternatives).
458    3. Compare design or policy variants using clearance, peak density, and bottleneck persistence
459       metrics.

485   9.8   Practical Design Implications

486     The results indicate that corridor-dominant geometries may require additional exit distribution
487   to reduce bottleneck persistence. Open-hall layouts demonstrate more balanced exit utilization
488   under congestion-aware routing strategies. The proposed framework can be used to compare
489   different layout options during the design stage and to identify configurations that may lead to
490   congestion or delayed evacuation. It may also be useful for testing the impact of changes such as
491   exit placement, corridor width, or occupancy levels. In this way, the approach provides a simple
492   way to explore “what-if” scenarios without requiring detailed manual simulation setup.

493   9.9   Policy and Regulatory Implications

494     PeopleFlow is intended to complement, not replace, prescriptive compliance workflows. In
495   practice, the framework can serve as an evidence layer for performance-based safety review
496   by quantifying congestion hotspots, blocked-exit sensitivity, and clearance robustness under
497   standardized stress scenarios. This supports transparent communication between designers,
498   safety engineers, and authorities by adding reproducible scenario evidence to conventional rule-
499   based checks, especially for high-occupancy facilities where geometry-induced bottlenecks can
500   dominate risk. For jurisdictional review workflows, these outputs are suitable as supplementary
501   technical evidence for Fire Marshal and Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ) discussions in
502   performance-based evacuation assessments.


      10    LIMITATIONS

503   This study has five main limitations. First, simulations are 2D and do not represent vertical
504   circulation through stairs or elevators. A direct multi-floor extension would model each floor as a
505   graph layer and encode stairs/elevators as inter-layer edges; the cost-function logic in Eq. (5) can
506   then be evaluated on this augmented graph with additional vertical-transition penalties. Recent
507   high-rise evacuation-elevator experiments further motivate this extension Mossberg et al. (2020).
508   Second, hazard fields such as smoke and heat are not yet coupled to route choice in a physically
509   calibrated way. Third, panic behavior is simplified, and human behavioral adaptation such as
510   group cohesion and leader-following dynamics are approximated; group-level coordination and
511   emergency communication effects are not explicitly modeled, which may influence evacuation
512   dynamics in real deployments. Fourth, automatic floor-plan extraction can require correction
513   for ambiguous drawings. Fifth, field-scale validation is currently limited to consistency checks
514   against literature ranges rather than controlled human-subject evacuation trials. Supplementary
515   airport/metro/office evaluations were expanded to full five-scenario coverage in this revision, but
516   broader real-layout cohorts are still needed for stronger external generalization. These limitations
517   define clear directions for future expansion and do not affect the comparative validity of the
518   presented experiments.

519   Future work will focus on (1) multi-floor 3D evacuation with staircase dynamics, (2) coupling

521   under hazards, and (4) live digital twin integration with sensor streams for online risk assessment.
522   For advanced routing-policy research, future extensions can also incorporate preference-aware
523   and compilation-based multi-agent pathfinding formulations to improve decision robustness in
524   constrained networks Ho and Nakadai (2022); Surynek (2022). We also plan to expand the open
525   benchmark set with additional plan categories such as hospital emergency floors, mall atria,
526   railway concourses, and auditorium layouts for stronger cross-domain generalization, and to
527   perform additional validation using controlled evacuation drills or publicly available trajectory
528   datasets.

529 Practical Implications: For near-term deployment, the most actionable recommendation is to
530 use PeopleFlow as a comparative screening tool during concept and retrofit stages: run structured
531 stress scenarios early, rank alternatives by congestion-risk indicators and clearance robustness,
532 and forward only the strongest candidates into detailed engineering review. This workflow reduces
533 redesign latency and improves traceability of safety decisions.



      12   CONCLUSION

534   This paper presented a reproducible geometry-aware multi-agent framework for structured evalua-
535   tion of building evacuation safety performance. Across a 450-run core matrix and supplementary
536   parametric/generalization experiments, we showed substantial sensitivity of evacuation outcomes
537   to geometric topology and routing assumptions, including an observed 8.27x range in clearance
538   time and order-of-magnitude variation in peak density. Ablation, parameter-sensitivity, and
539   method-comparison experiments demonstrate that density-sensitive path-selection policies and
540   scenario design materially affect measured safety outcomes in structured safety-evaluation settings.

541     The work advances evacuation research by framing simulation as an auditable, reproducible
542   framework rather than a one-off prediction tool. The resulting computational workflow supports
543   transparent comparison for architects, safety engineers, and smart-city planners who need evidence-
544   based prioritization of safer design and policy options. Overall, PeopleFlow provides a practical
545   foundation for experiment-driven evacuation safety evaluation, supports data-driven infrastructure
546   design, and serves as a pre-deployment evidence layer for standards-aligned review, design iteration,
547   and operational preparedness assessment.

548     PeopleFlow supports early-stage evacuation planning by enabling rapid evaluation of archite-
549   ctural design alternatives prior to construction. The framework can assist safety engineers in
550   identifying congestion-prone regions, evaluating exit placement strategies, and testing emerge-
551   ncy routing policies within digital twin environments. Such simulation-driven design assessment
552   can contribute to safer high-occupancy infrastructure including transportation hubs, hospitals,
553   and commercial facilities. The workflow aligns with emerging digital twin methodologies for
554   safety-aware infrastructure modeling.
